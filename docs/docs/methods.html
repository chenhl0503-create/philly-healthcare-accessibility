Data & Methods

1. Data overview

1.1 Supply-side data

The supply table lists all datasets that describe where care is provided:

  • Hospitals and acute-care facilities. Core supply points, merged from OpenDataPhilly, state health agencies, CMS and OpenStreetMap, then de-duplicated by name and address.
  • City and community health centers. Safety-net providers scraped from the Philadelphia Department of Public Health website to fill gaps in official open data.
  • Buffer-area hospitals. Facilities in surrounding counties that fall within the network catchment of Philadelphia tracts, these are essential for correcting edge effects.
  • Hospital bed capacity. Used as a proxy for acute-care capacity when computing supply-to-demand ratios in the E2SFCA model.

1.2 Demand-side data

Demand-side datasets describe who needs care and how vulnerable they are:

  • Total population by tract (ACS 2021). Pulled via the U.S. Census API and used as baseline demand in all accessibility calculations.
  • Social Vulnerability Index (SVI, CDC ATSDR). A tract-level composite index in the range 0â€“1 capturing socioeconomic status, minority status, housing and transportation vulnerability.
  • Medicaid share. Derived from ACS insurance tables and used to approximate the share of residents relying on public insurance.
  • WorldPop population grid. A high-resolution raster downloaded manually from WorldPop.org and used to cross-check ACS population counts through zonal statistics.

1.3 Basic spatial and network data

Spatial and network layers provide the geographic backbone for the model:

  • Census tract boundaries, including neighbors. TIGER/Line shapefiles, downloaded and processed programmatically, with an extended region covering neighboring counties for edge-effect correction.
  • Road network (driving graph). A drivable street network extracted from OpenStreetMap via OSMnx, including speed estimates and travel times.
  • Travel-time OD matrix (minutes). Originâ€“destination travel times in minutes between tract centroids and facilities, computed on the OSMnx graph.
  • HUD Qualified Census Tracts (QCT). Additional vulnerability layer to identify historically disadvantaged tracts.
  • City and county boundary. Used only for cartographic masking, to avoid artificially truncating catchments at the study-area border.

1.4 Data acquisition methods

(a) APIs and web services

  • U.S. Census Bureau API. requests is used to download ACS 2021 5-year estimates for population and Medicaid-related insurance variables at the tract level.
  • ArcGIS REST API (HUD QCT). Qualified Census Tracts are queried directly as GeoJSON, filtered to the extended Philadelphia region, and saved to disk for reproducibility.
  • Nominatim geocoding API. Geocodes scraped health center addresses into coordinates using geopy, then projected and snapped to the street network.

(b) Web scraping

  • BeautifulSoup scraper for city health centers. Some safety-net facilities are only listed on an HTML page, a small script parses the table, standardizes addresses, and writes a clean CSV before geocoding.

(c) Specialized Python libraries

  • OSMnx. Downloads the drivable road network, adds edge speeds and travel times, and provides nearest-node mapping from centroids and facilities to the graph.
  • GeoPandas and Rasterio. Used to read shapefiles, project geometries, and perform zonal statistics on the WorldPop raster to validate ACS populations.

(d) Static archives and manual downloads

  • WorldPop raster (usa_pop_2020...tif). Downloaded manually from WorldPop.org, stored under data/raw/, read inside a helper function (process_worldpop_raster). There is no download script in this repository.
  • CDC SVI CSV (philly_svi_2020.csv). Downloaded manually from CDC/ATSDR, then read via pd.read_csv(DATA_RAW / "philly_svi_2020.csv").

  1. Methods 2.1 Preprocessing and integration

Cleaning and standardising IDs. All tract-level datasets (ACS, SVI, QCT) are keyed by GEOID, facility datasets are keyed by stable identifiers. Duplicates and obvious geocoding errors are removed.

Merging supply layers. Hospitals, buffer-area hospitals, and community health centers are merged into a unified facility layer with type, Medicaid acceptance, and bed capacity.

Validating population counts. WorldPop raster counts are aggregated to tracts and compared to ACS totals, ACS remains the authoritative source in the final model.

Constructing vulnerability and insurance indicators. SVI (RPL_THEMES) is cleaned and re-scaled to 0â€“1, Medicaid share is derived from ACS insurance tables.
